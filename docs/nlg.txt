Natural language generation (NLG) is software technology that produces natural language output. A well-known survey defines NLG as “the subfield of artificial intelligence and computational linguistics concerned with building computer systems that can produce understandable texts in English or other human languages from underlying non-linguistic information.”

NLG powers applications like weather or patient reports, image captions, and chatbots such as ChatGPT. Essentially, it’s the automated version of what humans do when turning ideas into speech or writing—a process called language production in psycholinguistics. NLG systems can also be compared to tools like decompilers or transpilers that translate code into human-readable forms, though natural languages are far more complex and ambiguous than programming languages, making NLG significantly more challenging.

NLG complements natural-language understanding (NLU). In NLU, systems analyze and disambiguate human language input to produce a structured machine-readable form. By contrast, NLG starts with precise internal data and decides how to express it in words. NLU must handle ambiguous or error-prone user input, while NLG focuses on generating clear, coherent text from known information. However, NLG still faces the challenge of choosing among many possible ways to phrase the same idea while ensuring consistency and fluency.

NLG has existed since the 1960s, starting with early systems like ELIZA, but commercial use took off in the 1990s. Techniques range from simple template-based methods, such as mail merges generating form letters, to advanced systems that model human grammar and style. Modern NLG increasingly uses machine learning, training statistical models on large collections of human-written text to produce more natural and flexible language output.
